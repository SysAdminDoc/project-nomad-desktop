Essential Knots

Bowline — Non-slip loop. Rescue, anchoring. "Rabbit comes out of the hole, around the tree, back down the hole."
Clove Hitch — Quick attach to post/pole. Adjustable. Starting knot for lashings.
Taut-Line Hitch — Adjustable loop for tent guy lines. Slides to tighten, holds under load.
Square Knot — Join two ropes of equal size. Bandages, packages. Right over left, left over right.
Sheet Bend — Join two ropes of different sizes. Stronger than square knot.
Figure Eight — Stopper knot. Prevents rope from pulling through. Easy to untie.
Trucker's Hitch — 3:1 mechanical advantage. Tie-downs, load securing. The "king of knots."
Prusik Knot — Slides when unloaded, grips under weight. Ascending rope, rescue.

Common Medication Dosages

Ibuprofen (Advil/Motrin) — 200-400mg every 4-6h. Max 1200mg/day OTC. Anti-inflammatory + pain + fever.
Acetaminophen (Tylenol) — 500-1000mg every 4-6h. Max 3000mg/day. Pain + fever. NO alcohol.
Diphenhydramine (Benadryl) — 25-50mg every 4-6h. Allergies + sleep + mild nausea. Causes drowsiness.
Loperamide (Imodium) — 4mg initial, then 2mg per loose stool. Max 8mg/day OTC. Anti-diarrheal.
Aspirin — 325-650mg every 4h for pain. 81-325mg for suspected heart attack (chew, don't swallow).
Pseudoephedrine (Sudafed) — 30-60mg every 4-6h. Nasal decongestant. Raises BP.
Guaifenesin (Mucinex) — 200-400mg every 4h. Loosens mucus. Drink lots of water.
Bismuth subsalicylate (Pepto) — 30mL or 2 tabs every 30-60min. Max 8 doses/day. Stomach/nausea/diarrhea.
Hydrocortisone 1% — Apply thin layer 2-4x/day. Rash, itch, insect bites. Max 7 days on face.
Bacitracin/Neosporin — Apply to clean wound 1-3x/day. Prevents infection.
Shelf life: Most OTC meds retain 90%+ potency for 1-5 years past expiration if stored cool and dry. Exceptions: nitroglycerin, insulin, liquid antibiotics, EpiPens.

Improvised Survival Solutions

DIY Water Filter (Bio-Sand)
Layer in a container (bucket, bottle, pipe) from bottom up:
1. Gravel (2") — drainage layer
2. Coarse sand (4") — large particle removal
3. Fine sand (8") — bacterial/sediment removal
4. Activated charcoal (2") — chemical/taste removal
5. Fine sand (2") — prevent charcoal from floating
6. Gravel (1") — top layer to prevent channeling
Still requires boiling or chemical treatment after filtering to kill viruses.
Solar Water Still
1. Dig hole 3ft wide, 2ft deep in sunny area
2. Place collection container in center
3. Add vegetation, dirty water around container (not in it)
4. Cover hole with clear plastic sheet
5. Seal edges with dirt/rocks
6. Place small rock on plastic directly above container to form cone
7. Condensation forms, drips into container
Produces 1-2 quarts/day in good sun. Emergency method only.
Zeer Pot (Evaporative Fridge)
1. Get two unglazed clay pots (one fits inside the other)
2. Fill gap between pots with wet sand
3. Place food in inner pot
4. Cover with wet cloth
5. Place in shaded, breezy location
6. Keep sand wet — re-wet 2x daily
Keeps food 10-20F cooler than ambient. Best in dry climates. Can preserve produce for weeks.
Basic Snares
Simple loop snare: Wire (20-24 gauge) or paracord inner strands. Make loop 4" diameter for rabbits, fist-sized for squirrel. Secure to stake/tree at animal trail height.
Placement: Animal trails, near burrows, water sources. Set 6-12 snares for realistic catch rates. Check every 12 hours minimum.
Trigger snare: Bent sapling + trigger stick + snare. Sapling tension lifts and holds animal.
Check local laws. In true survival, protein trapping is 10x more efficient than hunting.
Ash Lye Soap
1. Collect hardwood ash (not softwood). Fill bucket.
2. Pour boiling water through ash, collect dark brown liquid (lye water)
3. Test strength: raw egg should float halfway
4. Heat animal fat/lard, strain clean
5. Mix 1 part lye water to 2 parts melted fat at ~100F
6. Stir 30+ minutes until trace (pudding consistency)
7. Pour into mold, cure 4-6 weeks
CAUTION: Lye is caustic. Avoid skin/eye contact. Work outdoors.
Dakota Fire Hole (Smokeless)
1. Dig main fire pit: 1ft wide, 1ft deep
2. Dig air tunnel: Start 1ft away, angle down to connect at bottom of fire pit
3. Tunnel opening should face INTO the wind
4. Build fire in main hole
Benefits: Nearly smokeless, hidden from view, wind-resistant, efficient fuel use, easy to cook over (pot sits on top), easy to extinguish (fill with dirt).
Best concealment fire method. Great for tactical situations or fire-ban conditions.

Navigation Without GPS

Finding North
Watch method: Point hour hand at sun. Halfway between hour hand and 12 = south (northern hemisphere). Reverse for southern hemisphere.
Shadow stick: Plant stick in ground. Mark shadow tip. Wait 15 min. Mark new tip. Line between marks runs East-West (first mark = west).
Night (N. hemisphere): Find Big Dipper. Front two stars point to Polaris (North Star). Polaris is within 1 degree of true north.
Night (S. hemisphere): Southern Cross. Extend long axis 4.5x its length toward horizon = approximate south.
Pace Counting (Distance)
Count double-steps (every time your LEFT foot hits ground). Average person = 62-68 paces per 100 meters on flat terrain.
Calibrate yourself: Walk a known 100m distance 3 times, count paces, average them.
Terrain adjustments: Uphill +10%, brush +15%, snow/sand +20%, night +25%
Ranger beads: Use a bead string — slide one bead per 100m. After 1km (10 beads), slide one top bead and reset bottom.
Compass Basics
Declination: Difference between magnetic north and true north. Varies by location. East declination: subtract from reading. West declination: add to reading.
Following a bearing: Set desired degree on bezel. Hold compass level. Rotate body until needle aligns with orienting arrow. Walk toward direction-of-travel arrow.
Back azimuth: Add 180 degrees to your bearing (or subtract if >180). Used to verify your back trail.

Night Sky Navigation

Finding Polaris (North Star)
1. Find the Big Dipper (7 bright stars, ladle shape)
2. The two stars forming the outer edge of the "cup" are "pointer stars"
3. Draw a line through them, extending 5x their separation
4. That line hits Polaris — the last star in the Little Dipper's handle
5. Polaris is within 1 degree of true north, all year
Southern Hemisphere
1. Find the Southern Cross (4 bright stars in cross shape)
2. Extend the long axis 4.5x its length toward the horizon
3. Drop straight down to the horizon = approximate south
Using Orion (visible globally)
Orion's belt rises due east, sets due west (within a few degrees)
The sword hangs south in N. hemisphere, north in S. hemisphere
Moon Navigation
Crescent moon: line through the horns to the horizon = south (N. hemisphere)
Full moon rises east at sunset, is south at midnight, sets west at sunrise

Game Processing Quick Reference

Field Dressing (Deer/Large Game)
1. Place animal on slope with head uphill if possible
2. Cut around anus, tie off with string to prevent contamination
3. Open abdomen from pelvis to ribcage — cut SKIN ONLY (don't puncture organs)
4. Cut diaphragm free from ribs on both sides
5. Reach up into chest, cut windpipe and esophagus as high as possible
6. Roll animal on side, pull organs out in one mass
7. Save heart and liver in separate bag if desired
8. Drain body cavity, prop open with stick for cooling
9. Get meat cooled below 40F as quickly as possible
Meat Preservation (No Refrigeration)
Jerky: Slice with grain, 1/4" thick. Salt heavily. Dry in sun/smoke 12-24 hrs until brittle.
Smoking: Cold smoke (80-90F) for preservation, hot smoke (160F+) for cooking. Hardwood only (no pine/resin).
Salt cure: Pack in salt (1 lb salt per 4 lbs meat). Store cool for 2-4 weeks. Rinse before cooking.
Pemmican: Dried jerky ground to powder + rendered fat (50/50 by weight). Add dried berries. Lasts months-years.

Emergency Protocols Quick Reference

CPR -- Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation (Adult) ▶
  1. Check scene safety. Make sure the area is safe for you and the victim.
  2. Check responsiveness. Tap shoulders firmly, shout "Are you okay?"
  3. Call 911 (or have someone call). Get an AED if available.
  4. Check breathing. Look for chest rise for no more than 10 seconds. Gasping is NOT normal breathing.
  5. Begin chest compressions. Place heel of one hand on center of chest (lower half of sternum), other hand on top, fingers interlocked.
  6. Push hard and fast. Compress at least 2 inches deep, rate of 100-120/min (tempo of "Stayin' Alive").
  7. Allow full chest recoil between compressions. Don't lean on chest.
  8. 30 compressions : 2 breaths ratio. Tilt head back, lift chin, pinch nose, give 1-second breaths watching for chest rise.
  9. Continue until: AED arrives, EMS takes over, victim starts breathing, or you're physically unable.
  10. AED: Turn on, follow voice prompts. Apply pads. "Clear" before shock. Resume CPR immediately after shock.
Hands-Only CPR: If untrained or uncomfortable with breaths, continuous chest compressions at 100-120/min is still effective.
Severe Bleeding Control ▶
  1. Ensure your safety. Wear gloves if available. Avoid contact with blood.
  2. Apply direct pressure. Use gauze, cloth, or clothing pressed firmly on the wound. Do NOT remove — add more on top.
  3. If limb wound won't stop: Apply a tourniquet 2-3 inches above the wound (NOT on a joint). Tighten until bleeding stops.
  4. Note the time tourniquet was applied. Write "TQ" and time on patient's forehead or the tourniquet.
  5. Wound packing: For deep wounds (junctional areas like groin, armpit, neck), pack tightly with hemostatic gauze, then apply pressure.
  6. Pressure dressing: Wrap wound firmly with elastic bandage or Israeli bandage. Tight enough to control bleeding but check circulation below.
  7. Elevate the injured area above the heart if possible (unless suspected fracture).
  8. Treat for shock: Lay flat, elevate legs, keep warm with blankets, keep calm and still.
Remember: You will NOT hurt someone by applying a tourniquet. A limb can survive 6+ hours. Blood loss kills in minutes.
Water Purification Methods ▶
  1. Boiling (most reliable): Bring water to a rolling boil for 1 minute (3 minutes above 6,500 ft elevation). Let cool before drinking.
  2. Chemical treatment:
    • Household bleach (6-8.25% sodium hypochlorite, unscented): 8 drops per gallon of clear water, 16 drops if cloudy. Wait 30 minutes. Should have slight chlorine smell.
    • Iodine tablets: Follow package directions. Not for pregnant women or those with thyroid conditions.
    • Water purification tablets (Aquatabs, Katadyn): Follow package directions for dose and wait time.
  3. Filtration: Pump filters (0.2 micron or smaller) remove bacteria and protozoa. Does NOT remove viruses — combine with chemical treatment if viral contamination suspected.
  4. UV treatment: SteriPEN or similar. Effective against bacteria, viruses, and protozoa. Requires clear water and batteries.
  5. Solar disinfection (SODIS): Fill clear PET bottle, place in direct sunlight for 6+ hours (2 days if cloudy). Emergency backup method only.
Always pre-filter cloudy water through cloth, coffee filter, or sand filter BEFORE purifying.
Priority water sources: Rain > Springs > Fast streams > Lakes > Puddles. Avoid water near industrial/agricultural sites.
Emergency Shelter Construction ▶
  1. Site selection: High ground (avoid flood zones), natural windbreak, near water but not in a drainage. Avoid dead trees, hilltops (lightning), ravines (flash floods).
  2. Debris hut (no tools needed):
    • Find a sturdy ridgepole (9-12 ft long). Prop one end on a stump/rock at waist height.
    • Lean sticks along both sides at 45 degrees, creating a ribcage frame.
    • Pile leaves, pine needles, grass 2-3 feet thick over the frame.
    • Fill inside with dry leaves for insulation bedding.
    • Make it just big enough for your body — smaller = warmer.
  3. Tarp shelter (A-frame): Tie cordage between two trees. Drape tarp over ridgeline. Stake/weight corners at 45-degree angle. Face opening away from wind.
  4. Snow shelter: Dig into a drift or build a quinzhee (pile snow 5ft high, let settle 2 hours, hollow out). Poke ventilation hole. Sleep on elevated platform inside.
  5. Key principles: Insulation from ground is critical (use pine boughs, leaves, foam pad). Small entrance. Ventilation to prevent CO2 buildup. Wind protection.
The #1 killer in survival situations is exposure (hypothermia/hyperthermia). Shelter is your FIRST priority after immediate medical needs.
Fire Starting Methods ▶
  1. Gather materials FIRST:
    • Tinder: Dry grass, birch bark, cotton balls w/ vaseline, dryer lint, fatwood shavings, char cloth
    • Kindling: Pencil-thick dry sticks, split small wood
    • Fuel: Wrist-thick+ dry wood, graduated sizes
  2. Build the fire lay: Teepee method (lean kindling around tinder bundle) or log cabin (stack kindling in alternating layers around tinder).
  3. Ferro rod: Scrape rod with spine of knife at 45-degree angle, directing sparks onto tinder bundle. Most reliable method in wet conditions.
  4. Matches/lighter: Light tinder from upwind side, shield from wind with body. Waterproof matches > regular.
  5. Flint and steel: Strike steel against sharp flint edge, catch sparks on char cloth, transfer to tinder bundle, blow gently.
  6. Magnifying lens: Focus sunlight into tight point on dark tinder. Works only with direct sun.
  7. Fire maintenance: Feed gradually, larger fuel as fire grows. Keep dry wood supply stocked. Bank coals at night with ash for easy restart.
Always have a fire extinguishment plan. Clear area around fire. Never leave unattended. Build fire INSIDE shelter entrance, not inside enclosed shelter (CO poisoning).
Choking — Heimlich Maneuver ▶
  1. Confirm choking. Ask "Are you choking?" Look for: inability to speak/cough, hands at throat, turning blue.
  2. Adult/child (standing): Stand behind victim. Make a fist with one hand, place thumb side against abdomen above navel, below ribcage. Grasp fist with other hand. Give sharp upward thrusts.
  3. Repeat thrusts until object is expelled or victim becomes unconscious.
  4. If victim goes unconscious: Lower to ground carefully. Call 911. Begin CPR — look in mouth before each breath for the object. Remove if visible.
  5. Self-Heimlich: Make a fist above your navel. Drive it inward and upward against a chair back, countertop, or your other hand.
  6. Infant (under 1 year): Place face-down on your forearm, head lower than chest. Give 5 back blows between shoulder blades with heel of hand. Flip over, give 5 chest thrusts with 2 fingers on breastbone. Alternate until clear.
Never do blind finger sweeps in infants or children — you may push the object deeper.
Hypothermia Recognition & Treatment ▶
  1. Recognize stages:
    • Mild (90-95F/32-35C): Shivering, cold hands/feet, numbness, clumsiness, slurred speech
    • Moderate (82-90F/28-32C): Violent shivering then shivering STOPS, confusion, drowsiness, paradoxical undressing
    • Severe (below 82F/28C): No shivering, unconscious, very slow pulse, appears dead
  2. Remove from cold. Get to shelter. Remove wet clothing. Handle GENTLY — rough handling can cause cardiac arrest in severe cases.
  3. Passive rewarming (mild): Dry clothing, blankets, sleeping bag. Hot drinks (no alcohol/caffeine). Skin-to-skin contact in sleeping bag.
  4. Active rewarming (moderate): Warm water bottles/heat packs to neck, armpits, groin (major blood vessels). NOT hands/feet first.
  5. Severe hypothermia: Minimize movement. Rewarm torso FIRST. Keep horizontal. Even if no pulse detected, begin CPR — cold hearts can restart. Evacuate.
Do NOT give alcohol — it dilates blood vessels and increases heat loss. Do NOT rub extremities. Do NOT place in hot bath (cardiac shock risk).
Wound Closure & Infection Prevention ▶
  1. Clean the wound thoroughly. Irrigate with clean water under pressure (syringe, squeeze bottle, punctured water bottle cap). Remove debris. This is the MOST important step for preventing infection.
  2. Assess if closure is appropriate: Close clean cuts less than 6-8 hours old. Do NOT close: animal bites, punctures, visibly contaminated wounds, wounds older than 12 hours — these need to drain.
  3. Butterfly strips / Steri-strips: Apply perpendicular to wound. Place strips 3mm apart. Apply one side, pull wound edges together, apply other side. Reinforce with lengthwise strips.
  4. Skin staples: Evert wound edges. Place staple perpendicular to wound. Squeeze stapler firmly. Space staples 5-7mm apart.
  5. Sutures (if trained): Simple interrupted sutures, 5mm from edge, 5mm apart. Enter perpendicular, exit perpendicular. Square knot, 4-5 throws.
  6. After closure: Apply antibiotic ointment. Cover with sterile dressing. Change dressing daily. Watch for infection signs: increasing redness, warmth, swelling, pus, red streaks, fever.
Signs of infection requiring urgent care: red streaks spreading from wound, fever over 101F, foul smell, rapid swelling, green/brown drainage.
Burn Treatment ▶
  1. Stop the burning process. Remove from heat source. Remove clothing/jewelry near burn (unless stuck to skin). Do NOT pull off stuck fabric.
  2. Classify the burn:
    • 1st degree: Red, painful, no blisters (sunburn). Treat at home.
    • 2nd degree: Blisters, very painful, red/mottled. Treat at home if small (<3 inches). Seek help if on face, hands, feet, joints, or circumferential.
    • 3rd degree: White/charred/leathery, may be painless (nerves destroyed). ALWAYS needs medical care.
  3. Cool the burn: Run cool (NOT ice cold) water over it for 10-20 minutes. Do NOT use ice, butter, toothpaste, or oil.
  4. Do NOT pop blisters. They protect against infection. If they pop on their own, clean gently and apply antibiotic ointment.
  5. Cover with sterile non-stick dressing. Wrap loosely. Do NOT use fluffy cotton (sticks to wound).
  6. Pain management: Ibuprofen + acetaminophen together. Keep burned area elevated if possible.
  7. Hydrate aggressively. Burns cause massive fluid loss. Drink electrolyte solution.
  8. Watch for infection: Increasing pain after 48h, fever, pus, foul smell, red streaks.
Rule of 9s (body surface area): Head 9%, each arm 9%, chest 18%, back 18%, each leg 18%, groin 1%. Burns over 20% BSA = life-threatening shock risk.
Fracture & Splinting ▶
  1. Suspect a fracture if: Deformity, swelling, bruising, inability to bear weight, grinding/crunching sensation, pain with movement.
  2. Do NOT attempt to realign the bone unless there is no pulse below the injury (circulation at risk).
  3. Splint in position found. Immobilize the joint ABOVE and BELOW the fracture site.
  4. Splint materials: SAM splint, boards, sticks, rolled magazines/newspapers, trekking poles, cardboard. Pad with clothing/towels.
  5. Secure with: Tape, cravats, torn cloth strips, belts, paracord. Firm but not so tight it cuts circulation.
  6. Check circulation below the splint every 15-30 minutes: pulse, sensation, skin color, capillary refill (press nail, color should return in <2 seconds).
  7. Elevate the injured limb above the heart if possible.
  8. Cold pack: Apply wrapped cold pack 20 min on, 20 min off to reduce swelling.
  9. Sling: For arm/clavicle fractures, use a triangular bandage or improvised sling + swathe to immobilize against body.
Open fracture (bone visible or skin broken): Cover wound with sterile dressing, splint around it, treat for shock, evacuate immediately. High infection risk.
Snake Bite / Envenomation ▶
  1. Move away from the snake. Do NOT try to catch or kill it. Note appearance if possible (color, pattern, head shape).
  2. Keep victim calm and still. Increased heart rate spreads venom faster. Lie down with bite site below heart level.
  3. Remove jewelry and watches near the bite — severe swelling will occur.
  4. Mark the edge of swelling with a pen and note the time. This tracks spread rate.
  5. Clean the wound gently with soap and water. Cover with clean dressing.
  6. Immobilize the bitten limb with a splint. Keep at or below heart level.
  7. Evacuate to medical facility. Antivenom is the definitive treatment. Call ahead so they can prepare.
Do NOT: Cut the wound, suck out venom, apply tourniquet, apply ice, give alcohol, apply electric shock. These all make it WORSE.
Most US venomous snakes: Rattlesnake, Copperhead, Cottonmouth (water moccasin), Coral snake. Pit vipers have triangular heads, vertical pupils, heat pits. "Red touches yellow, kills a fellow" (coral snake).
Allergic Reaction / Anaphylaxis ▶
  1. Recognize anaphylaxis: Difficulty breathing, throat/tongue swelling, widespread hives, dizziness, rapid pulse, vomiting, feeling of doom. Can progress to death in minutes.
  2. EpiPen (if available): Remove blue safety cap. Press orange tip firmly into outer thigh (through clothing is OK). Hold 10 seconds. Massage injection site.
  3. Call 911 immediately. Even after EpiPen, symptoms can return (biphasic reaction).
  4. Position: Sit upright if breathing difficulty. Lay flat with legs elevated if lightheaded/low BP. Recovery position if unconscious but breathing.
  5. Antihistamine: Give diphenhydramine (Benadryl) 50mg if they can swallow — this helps but is NOT a substitute for epinephrine in anaphylaxis.
  6. Second EpiPen: If no improvement after 5-15 minutes, give a second dose in the other thigh.
  7. Monitor continuously. Be prepared to start CPR if breathing stops or pulse is lost.
  8. Without EpiPen: Antihistamine, keep airway open, treat for shock, evacuate IMMEDIATELY. This is a true emergency.
Common triggers: Bee/wasp stings, peanuts/tree nuts, shellfish, medications (penicillin, NSAIDs), latex. If someone has a known allergy, ensure EpiPen is always accessible and not expired.
